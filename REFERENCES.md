## Housing Affordability (2024-2026)

These sources document the current housing crisis and validate the app's core thesis that housing has become dramatically less affordable relative to income.

- **Harvard JCHS State of the Nation's Housing 2025** - https://www.jchs.harvard.edu/press-releases/new-report-highlights-unease-housing-market-amid-worsening-affordability-crisis
  - Key takeaway: Median existing home price hit $412,500 in 2024, a "shocking five times the median household income." Price-to-income ratio of 3 has traditionally been considered affordable. Home sales at a 30-year low.
  - Relevant to: Act 1 hero stat, housing item card, year context for 2024-2025

- **CBRE: Digging Out of the Housing Affordability Crisis** - https://www.cbreim.com/insights/articles/digging-out-of-the-us-housing-affordability-crisis
  - Key takeaway: Income needed to buy a single-family home has doubled since 2019. Owning a median-priced home consumes 47.7% of median household income. Median age of first-time buyer is now 40 (was 33 five years ago).
  - Relevant to: Act 1 housing cards, "American Dream" preset context

- **Fortune: Affordability crisis driving unprecedented price cuts** - https://fortune.com/2026/02/12/housing-affordability-crisis-new-home-price-cuts-realtor-com-report/
  - Key takeaway: Lennar CEO calls it an "affordability crisis leaving many average American families feeling excluded from the traditional promise of upward mobility." Nearly 20% of new homes faced price cuts in Q4 2025.
  - Relevant to: Year context for 2025, validates the emotional framing of the app

- **NAHB: How affordability challenges affect housing trends 2026** - https://www.lbmjournal.com/industry-news/data/press-release/15817630/national-association-of-home-builders-how-affordability-challenges-are-affecting-housing-trends-in-2026
  - Key takeaway: Home prices up 53% since 2019, income up only 24%. First-time buyer share dropped to 21% (from 44% in 1981). Median first-time buyer age hit record 40 (was 29 in 1981).
  - Relevant to: Act 1 context cards, "American Dream" math

- **Bright MLS: What consumers think causes the crisis** - https://www.realestatenews.com/2026/02/10/what-consumers-think-is-causing-the-housing-affordability-crisis
  - Key takeaway: 55.5% say people "do not earn enough money to afford a home." 50.1% say mortgage rates too high. Consumers and economists disagree on root causes.
  - Relevant to: Act 1 intro copy, validates the "your year buys less" framing

- **HousingWire: Housing affordability set to recover** - https://www.housingwire.com/articles/housing-affordability-2026/
  - Key takeaway: Price-to-income ratio peaked above 5x in 2022, easing to ~4.9x by end of 2026. Historically, 4x signals affordable. We are three years into a slow recovery.
  - Relevant to: Year context for 2022-2025, adds nuance that things may be slowly improving

- **CNBC: Americans muscling through affordability crisis** - https://www.cnbc.com/2025/12/26/how-americans-are-responding-to-the-affordability-crisis.html
  - Key takeaway: 70% of Americans say cost of living is "not affordable" for average family. 29% dipped into savings in Q4 2025. Coffee up 19%, beef up 15%, housing up 14% (2023-2025).
  - Relevant to: Grocery item cards context, overall app messaging validation

- **TCW: Resetting the Baseline** - https://www.tcw.com/Insights/2025/2025-12-18-The-Long-View
  - Key takeaway: Monthly mortgage payments on median home rose to $2,570 (record). Need $126,700 income to afford it. Only 6 million of 46 million renters can meet this benchmark. Median buyer age surged to 59 years old.
  - Relevant to: Housing affordability calculations, Act 1 context

## Wealth Inequality & Concentration (2024-2026)

These sources provide the data and framing for Act 2's wealth concentration narrative.

- **CBS News: Wealth inequality hits widest gap in 3 decades** - https://www.cbsnews.com/news/us-wealth-gap-widest-in-three-decades-federal-reserve/
  - Key takeaway: Top 1% held ~$55 trillion in Q3 2025, roughly equal to bottom 90% combined. Billionaire wealth grew 3x faster than average rate over prior 5 years. Top 10% account for nearly half of all consumer spending.
  - Relevant to: Act 2 wealth stats cards, key statistics

- **CNBC: K-shaped economy more striking than ever** - https://www.cnbc.com/2026/01/30/wealth-inequality-k-shaped-economy-united-states-consumer-spending-trump.html
  - Key takeaway: Top 1% net worth hit record 32% share in Q3 2025. Bottom 50% held 2.5%. Worker compensation share of GDP at lowest level in 75+ years. Layoffs surged 50% in 2025.
  - Relevant to: Act 2 productivity vs wages framing, wealth stats

- **World Inequality Report 2026** - https://wir2026.wid.world/insight/global-economic-inequity/
  - Key takeaway: Top 0.001% (~56,000 people) own more wealth than bottom 50% of world (2.8 billion people). Their share grew from 3.7% in 1995 to 6.1% in 2025. Billionaires enjoyed 8% annual wealth growth.
  - Relevant to: Act 2 billionaire cards, global context

- **Inequality.org: Wealth Inequality** - https://inequality.org/facts/wealth-inequality/
  - Key takeaway: Top 12 US billionaires surpass $2.7 trillion combined (quadrupled since March 2020). 100 billionaire families spent $2.6 billion on 2024 elections (16.5% of all political contributions, up from 0.6% in 2000).
  - Relevant to: Act 2 billionaire wealth comparison, political context

- **Inequality.org: Income Inequality** - https://inequality.org/facts/income-inequality/
  - Key takeaway: Top 10% account for 49.2% of consumer spending (up from 43.2% in 2020). 10th-percentile wage up 28.6% since 1979; 90th-percentile up 64.1%. Bottom 90% income share declined for decades.
  - Relevant to: Act 2 income divergence chart, wage growth data

- **Oxfam: Billionaire wealth share** - https://www.oxfamamerica.org/explore/issues/economic-justice/what-percent-of-the-worlds-wealth-is-controlled-by-billionaires/
  - Key takeaway: 340 new billionaires in 2025. 10 richest US billionaires gained ~$700B in one year. 791 million workers saw wages fail to keep pace with inflation (2021-2023), losing ~25 days of wages per worker.
  - Relevant to: Act 2 billionaire cards, "where did the money go" transition copy

- **Al Jazeera: World Inequality Report 2026 summary** - https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2025/12/10/where-in-the-world-are-wealth-and-income-most-unequal
  - Key takeaway: Richest 10% own 75% of all personal wealth. US top 1% take 20% of income, bottom 50% take 10%. In Europe, top 1% takes only 12% and bottom 50% takes 22%.
  - Relevant to: Act 2, potential international comparison feature

## Historical Context by Era

Use these for writing YearContext blurbs and validating historical data.

### Pre-1920s
- **BLS Bulletin No. 604** - Historical wage data for manufacturing workers
  - Relevant to: Pre-1940 income estimates in the JSON

### 1920s-1930s (Depression Era)
- Key facts for context cards: Unemployment hit 25% in 1933. Bank failures wiped out savings. Housing prices fell ~30% but incomes fell faster, so homes were actually LESS affordable (ratio of 3.1x vs 2.3x in 1950).

### 1940s-1960s (Post-War Boom)
- Key facts: GI Bill made college free for veterans. FHA loans democratized homeownership. Single income could support a family of four. This is the era people are nostalgic for, and the numbers back it up.

### 1970s-1980s (Stagflation & Divergence)
- Key facts: 1979 is when productivity and wages started diverging. Oil crises. Inflation peaked at 14.8% in 1980. Interest rates hit 18%+. Despite high rates, home prices were still only ~3x income.

### 1990s-2000s (Dot-Com & Housing Bubble)
- Key facts: CEO pay ratio exploded from 58:1 (1989) to 344:1 (2000s). Housing bubble inflated prices. College costs began accelerating past inflation.

### 2010s-Present
- Key facts: Post-2008 recovery was K-shaped. Home prices recovered faster than wages. Student debt crossed $1 trillion. Pandemic stimulus temporarily boosted bottom earners, but gains reversed by 2025.

## Cultural Discourse & Framing

These inform the app's tone and validate that we're tapping into a real cultural conversation, not creating one.

- The phrase "affordability crisis" dominates current headlines and political campaigns
- 70% of Americans say cost of living is "not affordable" for the average family (Marist poll, Dec 2025)
- First-time homebuyer median age is now 40, was 29 in 1981
- More than half of adults 18-24 now live with their parents
- Consumer confidence gap between highest and lowest earners is at widest point in a decade

**Framing principles for UI copy:**
- We are not the first to observe this problem. We are giving people a tool to see it clearly.
- The "bootstraps" narrative fails when the math doesn't work. Show the math.
- Avoid implying a solution. Present data. Let users draw conclusions.
- The most powerful emotional trigger is the generational comparison: "Your grandparents weren't smarter with money."
